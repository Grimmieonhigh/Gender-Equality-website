Education access for girls Worldwide

Approximately 130 million girls worldwide are out of school, facing poverty, child marriage, gender-based violence despite education being a right. This highlights the reality that despite having the same curiosity, intelliigence as boys, girls are still often denied the opportunity to learn and grow.

This issue is not only about education but about equality, opportunity and basic human rights. When girls are prevented from accessing education, it reinforces the harmful belief that their futures are less important. Over time, this lack of access impacts their confidence, independence and the ability to make choices of their lives.

To me, this is deeply unfair and upsetting. No one should be denied an education because of their gender. Girls everywhere deserve the same chance to learn, succeed, and shape their own futures, yet for many this remains out of reach.

What makes it even more concerning is that this issue is often overlooked or normalised.

Many people are unaware of how widespread the problem is or how deeply it affects not just individuals, but entire communities over generations. That is why raising awareness and speaking about it is so important.

I strongly believe that everyone has a role creating a fairer and more equal world, and change is essential. Equal access to education for girls should be given, and the fact that it is not shows how much progress still needs to be made.

Key facts:

  • There have been significant gains in girls’ access to primary and secondary education. Globally, primary and secondary school enrollment rates are almost equal where 92% of boys are enrolled and 90% of girls. Almost two-thirds of all countries have reached gender parity in primary school enrollment.
  • girls are 2.5 times more likely to be out of primary school than boys
  • Since the Taliban retook power in 2021 in Afghanistan, girls’ right to education has been severely curtailed. Girls have been banned from secondary schools and higher education since 2022, and despite primary school enrolment still being allowed, nearly 30% of Afghan girls haven’t been to school.
  • Even in places where girls start school alongside boys, girls are much more likely to drop out of school as the years progress. This can be due to pressures around a lack of income for food, transport, school fees, uniforms, and essentials like menstrual products.

Why does this Happen?

There are numerous obstacles to girls' education, such as poverty, cultural norms, early marriage, and an absence of accessible or safe schools. Over time, these elements frequently come together to create barriers that keep girls from attending or completing their education. Many girls are expected to take on caregiving or household duties, which can make it difficult for them to concentrate in class or even go at all. Families sometimes prioritise the education of boys over that of girls, which contributes to the gap.

Real-world impact

The impact of the lack of access to education for girls is very real in the sense that it affects their independence, their future career paths, as well as their future security. Therefore, when girls are not given the opportunity to go to school, it becomes very difficult for them to lead stable lives in the future. In addition to the personal impact, this problem also affects the larger society in the sense that it creates inequalities.

How can we reduce it?

  • Providing scholarships and cash transfers to familes reduces the direct and indirect costs of schooling
  • Building schools closer to communities and constructing safe, toilets helps manage attendance and menstrual hygiene.
  • Community awareness programs to countering child marriage and encouraging families to prioritize girls' education.
  • Focusing on marginalized communities, including those with disabilities or in conflict zones, ensures no girl is left behind.
  • Promoting policies that ensure gender equality in curricula and school management.
  • Providing skills training and fostering confidence in girls to stay in education

What can you do?

You can help by raising awareness, supporting equal access to education initiatives, and speaking out against inequality whenever you see it. Encourage open conversations about education opportunities and the barriers girls face, support and uplift girls in your community, and hold organisations and governments accountable so they can create safe, inclusive, and supportive learning environments. Every small or big action contributes to building a more equal future for everyone.
